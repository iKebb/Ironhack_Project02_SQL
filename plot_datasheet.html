Visualization Guide by Analysis Type

1. Univariate Plots

What it shows: The distribution of a single variable.

When to use: During initial data exploration to understand central tendency, spread, skewness, and presence of outliers. Essential before any modeling or comparison.

Histogram – Continuous Distribution
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6)) plt.hist(df['Track Score'], bins=50, color='#e94560', alpha=0.7, edgecolor='white') plt.title('Distribution of Track Score') plt.xlabel('Track Score') plt.ylabel('Frequency') plt.show()
KDE Plot – Smooth Density Estimate
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6)) sns.kdeplot(df['Spotify Popularity'].dropna(), shade=True, color='#ff9a76') plt.title('Density of Spotify Popularity') plt.xlabel('Popularity (0–100)') plt.show()
Boxplot – Outlier Detection
plt.figure(figsize=(8, 5)) sns.boxplot(y=df['YouTube Views'], color='#ffb3a7') plt.title('YouTube Views Distribution & Outliers') plt.ylabel('Views') plt.yscale('log') plt.show()
Countplot – Categorical Frequencies
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6)) sns.countplot(x='Explicit Track', data=df, palette=['#e94560', '#ff9a76']) plt.title('Count of Explicit vs Non-Explicit Tracks') plt.xlabel('Explicit (1 = Yes)') plt.show()
Use univariate plots first in any analysis pipeline. They reveal data quality issues (e.g., unexpected zeros, extreme skew) and guide decisions on transformations or imputation.

2. Bivariate Plots

What it shows: The relationship between two variables.

When to use: To explore associations, correlations, or group differences (e.g., how track score varies by explicit content, or how streams correlate across platforms).

Scatter Plot – Numeric vs Numeric
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6)) sns.scatterplot(x='Spotify Streams', y='YouTube Views', data=df, alpha=0.6, color='#e94560') plt.xscale('log'); plt.yscale('log') plt.title('Spotify Streams vs YouTube Views') plt.show()
Barplot – Numeric vs Categorical
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6)) sns.barplot(x='Explicit Track', y='Track Score', data=df, ci=None, palette=['#ff9a76', '#ffb3a7']) plt.title('Average Track Score by Explicit Flag') plt.xlabel('Explicit (1 = Yes)') plt.ylabel('Avg Track Score') plt.show()
Boxplot by Category
plt.figure(figsize=(12, 6)) top_artists = df['Artist'].value_counts().head(10).index sns.boxplot(x='Artist', y='Spotify Popularity', data=df[df['Artist'].isin(top_artists)], palette='magma') plt.title('Spotify Popularity by Top 10 Artists') plt.xticks(rotation=45) plt.show()
Line Plot – Temporal Trend
df['Release Year'] = df['Release Date'].dt.year pop_by_year = df.groupby('Release Year')['Spotify Popularity'].mean() plt.figure(figsize=(12, 6)) plt.plot(pop_by_year.index, pop_by_year.values, color='#e94560', marker='o') plt.title('Average Spotify Popularity Over Time') plt.xlabel('Year') plt.ylabel('Avg Popularity') plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3) plt.show()
Bivariate visualizations answer questions like “Does X affect Y?” or “How do groups differ?”. Always consider log scales for skewed metrics like streams or views.

3. Multivariate Plots

What it shows: Relationships among three or more variables.

When to use: To uncover complex patterns, interactions, or cluster structures (e.g., how popularity, streams, and platform presence co-vary across artists or genres).

Pairplot – Multiple Numeric Relationships
subset = df[['Track Score', 'Spotify Popularity', 'YouTube Views', 'TikTok Views']].dropna() sns.pairplot(subset, plot_kws={'alpha': 0.5, 'color': '#e94560'}) plt.suptitle('Pairwise Relationships Among Key Metrics', y=1.02) plt.show()
Heatmap – Correlation Matrix
numeric_cols = df.select_dtypes(include=[np.number]).columns corr = df[numeric_cols].corr() plt.figure(figsize=(14, 12)) sns.heatmap(corr, cmap='coolwarm', center=0, annot=False, cbar_kws={'shrink': 0.8}, linewidths=0.5) plt.title('Correlation Matrix of Numeric Features') plt.show()
FacetGrid – Conditional Distributions
g = sns.FacetGrid(df[df['Artist'].isin(df['Artist'].value_counts().head(6).index)], col='Artist', col_wrap=3, sharex=True, sharey=True) g.map(sns.histplot, 'Track Score', bins=20, color='#ff9a76') g.set_titles('{col_name}') g.set_axis_labels('Track Score', 'Frequency') plt.tight_layout() plt.show()
Clustered Heatmap – Grouped Correlations
from scipy.cluster.hierarchy import linkage sns.clustermap(corr.fillna(0), method='ward', cmap='vlag', figsize=(12, 12), dendrogram_ratio=0.1, cbar_pos=(0.02, 0.8, 0.03, 0.18)) plt.suptitle('Clustered Correlation Heatmap', y=0.98) plt.show()
Multivariate plots reveal hidden structures but can become cluttered. Always subset to the most relevant variables. Use clustering or dimensionality reduction (e.g., PCA) for high-dimensional data.
Start with univariate → move to bivariate → explore multivariate. Let your questions guide the complexity.
